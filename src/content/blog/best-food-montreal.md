## ü•á Montreal Smoked Meat Sandwich ‚Äî Best Overall Dish in Montreal

**Total Score: 95.8 / 100**

There is no separating Montreal from its smoked meat. The sandwich, hand sliced brisket cured for ten days in a secret blend of spices, piled high between two slices of seedless rye bread with a stripe of yellow mustard, is the city's defining culinary achievement.

What makes Montreal smoked meat different from pastrami or corned beef is the process. The brisket is dry cured with more cracked peppercorn and savory spices like coriander, garlic, and mustard seed, then hot smoked and finally steamed until impossibly tender. The result is meat so delicate it must be sliced by hand with a knife.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

‚úì **Flavor Complexity:** The interplay between the peppery crust, the rich fat content, and the slight sweetness of the cure creates extraordinary depth

‚úì **Ingredient Quality:** Top delis cure their own meat on premises. The mandatory ten day curing process at places like Schwartz's has remained unchanged since 1928

‚úì **Cultural Significance:** Brought to Montreal by Romanian Jewish immigrants in the late 1800s. The dish became officially bilingual in 1987 when "smoked meat" was declared acceptable in both French and English

‚úì **Accessibility:** Available at delis across the city. A classic sandwich costs $10 to $15

‚úì **Value:** A full meal with fries, pickle, coleslaw, and a cherry cola for under $20 at legendary spots

**Where We Loved It Most:** Schwartz's Hebrew Delicatessen on Saint Laurent Boulevard (operating since 1928, with the same recipe and brick smokehouse accumulating over 95 years of buildup), and Reuben's Deli for a slightly more modern take.

**Verdict:** If you eat only one thing in Montreal, this is it. The smoked meat sandwich here will permanently recalibrate your understanding of deli food. Order it "medium fat" for the ideal balance of flavor and moisture. The lean is dry, the fat is indulgent, but the medium is perfection. Pair it with a black cherry cola, a half sour pickle, and fries for the complete experience.

## ü•à Montreal Bagel ‚Äî Best Breakfast Experience

**Total Score: 94.2 / 100**

Montreal bagels are nothing like their New York counterparts, and that is entirely the point. Smaller, denser, sweeter, and with a larger hole, the Montreal bagel is hand rolled, boiled in honey water, and baked in a wood fired oven until it develops a slight char and an irresistible chewiness.

The two legendary bakeries, St Viateur (since 1957) and Fairmount (since 1949), have been producing these rings around the clock for generations. Both use wood fired ovens that have been burning continuously for decades.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

‚úì **Flavor Complexity:** The honey water bath adds a subtle sweetness that balances the char from the wood fire. The exterior is slightly crisp while the interior remains dense and chewy

‚úì **Ingredient Quality:** Hand rolled one by one, boiled in honey water for 30 to 60 seconds, then baked on long wooden planks in wood burning ovens

‚úì **Cultural Significance:** Brought to Montreal by Polish Jewish immigrants in the early 1900s. The Montreal bagel has even been to outer space (astronaut Gregory Chamitoff brought bags of Fairmount bagels to the International Space Station)

‚úì **Accessibility:** Both legendary bakeries are open 24 hours a day, seven days a week. A dozen bagels costs approximately $12 to $15

‚úì **Value:** A bag of fresh sesame bagels still warm from the oven for under $15 is one of the great bargains in North American food

**Where We Loved It Most:** St Viateur Bagel on St Viateur Street in Mile End (featured on Anthony Bourdain's "No Reservations" and Netflix's "Somebody Feed Phil") and Fairmount Bagel (Canada's first bagel bakery, established in 1949).

**Verdict:** The Montreal bagel is best eaten within minutes of emerging from the oven, still warm, with nothing but a schmear of cream cheese if anything at all. Both St Viateur and Fairmount produce exceptional bagels with subtle differences: Fairmount tends slightly sweeter, St Viateur slightly more charred. Visit both, declare your allegiance, and prepare to defend it for life. Local tip: ask for sesame, as it has the highest turnover and will most likely be fresh.

Poutine is the dish that defines Qu√©b√©cois comfort food. Fresh cut fries, topped with squeaky cheese curds, smothered in rich brown gravy. On paper it sounds like a simple late night snack. In practice, when done correctly, it is a transformative culinary experience.

**Verdict:** Start with the classic poutine before venturing into the specialty versions. The fundamentals matter most here: fresh curds, proper fry technique, and gravy at the right temperature. ## üå≠ Steamie ‚Äî Best Street Food

**Total Score: 88.4 / 100**

The steamie (or steam√© in French) is Montreal's answer to the hot dog, and it is unlike any hot dog you have had elsewhere. **Key Tasting Highlights:**

‚úì **Flavor Complexity:** The combination of tangy mustard, sweet relish, and fresh cabbage creates a balanced flavor profile unique to Montreal

‚úì **Cultural Significance:** A working class tradition dating to the 1940s urbanization of Montreal, when street vendors needed to serve food quickly and efficiently to factory workers

‚úì **Accessibility:** Available at casse cro√ªtes throughout the city for $2 to $4 each

‚úì **Value:** Two steamies and fries for under $10 is one of the best budget meals in the city

**Where We Loved It Most:** Montreal Pool Room on Boulevard Saint Laurent (serving steamies since 1912, the second oldest restaurant in the city), and Decarie Hot Dog in Ville Saint Laurent (operating since 1969).

**Verdict:** Order your steamie "all dressed" (tout garni) for the authentic experience. The soft, steamed bun is essential; if you prefer more texture, ask for a "toastie" which is grilled instead. Montreal Pool Room is the legendary choice, a no frills counter where the recipe has not changed in over a century.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Food in Montreal

**1. What is the single best food to eat in Montreal?**

The Montreal smoked meat sandwich is the definitive Montreal food experience. Scored 95.8/100 in our evaluation, it is the city's most iconic dish and represents nearly a century of culinary tradition. Every visitor should try it at Schwartz's or another traditional deli.

**2. What makes Montreal bagels different from New York bagels?**

Montreal bagels are smaller, denser, sweeter, and have a larger hole. They are hand rolled, boiled in honey water (not plain water), and baked in wood fired ovens. The result is a chewier, slightly sweeter bagel with a hint of char. New York bagels are larger, fluffier, and boiled in plain water before being baked in standard ovens.

**3. How much does a meal in Montreal cost?**

Montreal is one of the most affordable food cities in North America. A smoked meat sandwich costs $10 to $15, poutine runs $8 to $14, a dozen bagels is under $15, and a steamie is $2 to $4. A full day of excellent eating can cost as little as $30 to $40.

**4. Is poutine really worth trying?**

Absolutely. While the combination of fries, cheese curds, and gravy may sound simple, a properly made poutine at a place like La Banquise is a revelation. The key is fresh cheese curds that squeak and hot gravy that slightly melts them. Do not judge poutine by fast food imitations.

**5. Where is the best poutine in Montreal?**

La Banquise on Rue Rachel East is the most famous, offering over 30 varieties and open 24 hours. Chez Claudette and Patati Patata are excellent alternatives with shorter waits. For a gourmet take, Au Pied de Cochon serves a legendary foie gras poutine.

**6. What time do Montrealers eat?**

Lunch typically runs from noon to 2 PM, and dinner service starts around 6 PM and can run until 10 PM or later. Many popular restaurants fill up quickly on weekends. Arriving before 7 PM can help avoid long waits. Late night options like La Banquise and the casse cro√ªtes are open until the early morning hours.

**7. Is it worth visiting Jean Talon Market?**

Yes, especially on weekends. Jean Talon is one of the largest public markets in North America, offering direct access to local producers, exceptional Quebec cheeses, fresh seafood, and prepared foods. It is the best place to experience the breadth of Qu√©b√©cois terroir in a single visit.

**8. What is tourti√®re and where can I try it?**

Tourti√®re is a traditional French Canadian meat pie filled with spiced ground pork and/or beef, encased in a flaky crust. It is traditionally served at Christmas but available year round at Qu√©b√©cois restaurants. The warming spices of cinnamon, clove, and allspice give it a distinctive flavor.

**9. What is a steamie and where can I get one?**

A steamie (steam√© in French) is a Montreal style hot dog where both the frankfurter and the bun are steamed until soft, then topped with mustard, onions, relish, and cabbage. Montreal Pool Room (since 1912) and Decarie Hot Dog (since 1969) are the legendary spots.

**10. Can I eat well in Montreal on a budget?**

Montreal may be the best budget food city in North America. A smoked meat sandwich for $12, a bag of fresh bagels for $12, a poutine for $10, and two steamies for $6 means you can eat extraordinarily well for under $40 a day without compromising on quality or authenticity.
