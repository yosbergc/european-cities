When it comes to culinary destinations, Naples stands in a league of its own. This is the city that gave the world pizza, transformed the humble tomato into a culinary icon, and turned street food into high art  all while staying fiercely rooted in tradition.

We spent over four weeks eating our way through the city  from the narrow alleys of the Quartieri Spagnoli to the seaside trattorias of Mergellina  tasting over 60 dishes across more than 30 restaurants, pizzerias, bakeries, and street carts.

## How We Evaluated

Every dish was scored using a 100-point system across six categories: flavor complexity (25%), ingredient quality (20%), cultural significance (15%), accessibility (15%), value for money (15%), and overall experience (10%).

We evaluated dishes at a minimum of three different locations  from Michelin-recognised spots to family-run holes-in-the-wall that have been serving the same recipes for generations.

## ü•à Sfogliatella  Best Pastry

<div class="score-badge">93.1 / 100</div>

Naples runs on espresso and sfogliatelle. This iconic pastry comes in two forms: the *riccia* (flaky, shell-shaped, crisp layered dough) and the *frolla* (smooth shortcrust, rounder shape). Both are filled with a warm semolina and ricotta cream scented with citrus and cinnamon.

The riccia is the showstopper. Biting through the shattering layers of dough into the sweet, slightly grainy ricotta filling is one of the great sensory experiences in European pastry.

**Where to eat it:** Pasticceria Attanasio near Stazione Centrale, Gran Caff√® Gambrinus on Piazza del Plebiscito.

<div class="verdict-box">
**Verdict:** The sfogliatella riccia is a masterclass in texture. Pair it with a strong Neapolitan espresso at a standing bar for the full local experience. The frolla is excellent too  but the riccia is what you came for.
</div>

## Pasta e Patate con Provola  Best Comfort Dish

<div class="score-badge">89.5 / 100</div>

This dish is Neapolitan *cucina povera*  poor man's cooking  at its absolute finest. A thick, starchy soup of pasta and potatoes, finished with stretchy smoked provola cheese that melts into silky threads throughout. On paper it sounds impossibly simple. In practice it delivers a richness that rivals dishes with ten times the ingredients.

**Where to eat it:** Trattoria Da Nennella in the Quartieri Spagnoli (loud, chaotic, utterly authentic), Osteria Pignatelli.

<div class="verdict-box">
**Verdict:** Don't overlook this dish because the ingredients sound basic. A cold-weather essential and one of those rare dishes where the whole is infinitely greater than the sum of its parts.
</div>

## Cuoppo Fritto  Best Street Food

<div class="score-badge">88.7 / 100</div>

The cuoppo is Naples in a paper cone. A mix of lightly fried seafood  calamari, shrimp, small fish  served piping hot from street-side fryers. The batter is thin and crisp, letting the freshness of the seafood come through in every bite. There's also a *terra* version filled with fried potato croquettes, arancini, and frittatine di pasta.

**Where to eat it:** Friggitoria Vomero, the street carts along Via Pignasecca, Casa Capasso on Via dei Tribunali.

<div class="verdict-box">
**Verdict:** The ultimate walk-and-eat snack. Grab one from a busy stall where the oil is fresh and the turnover is constant. A squeeze of lemon on top is non-negotiable.
</div>

### What is the difference between sfogliatella riccia and frolla?

The *riccia* has a flaky, shell-shaped exterior made from many thin layers of crispy dough. The *frolla* has a smooth shortcrust pastry shell with a rounder shape. Both contain the same semolina-ricotta filling, but the riccia is the more traditional and popular version.

### Is Naples street food safe?

Yes. Neapolitan street food culture is deeply established. Stick to busy stalls with high turnover  this ensures fresh oil and freshly cooked food. The cuoppo, pizza fritta, and frittatine di pasta are all safe and widely consumed by locals daily.
