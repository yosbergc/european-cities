## ü•à Arepa de Huevo ‚Äî Best Street Food Experience

**Total Score: 93.8 / 100**

Cartagena runs on arepas de huevo. This ingenious street food ‚Äî a golden fried corn cake stuffed with a whole egg (and sometimes seasoned ground beef) then fried again until the exterior shatters and the yolk remains perfectly runny ‚Äî is a masterclass in texture and technique that has been perfected over 200 years.

The preparation is deceptively technical: the arepa is first fried until it puffs, then carefully sliced open, filled with a raw egg, sealed, and returned to the hot oil. The result? A crispy golden shell giving way to a warm, soft center where the egg has just set. Getting the timing right so the yolk stays runny while the dough cooks through is nothing short of culinary alchemy.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

‚úì **Flavor Complexity:** The contrast between crispy corn dough, rich egg yolk, and optional spiced beef is extraordinary

‚úì **Ingredient Quality:** Best versions use freshly made corn dough and perfectly fresh eggs. The oil must be at the exact right temperature

‚úì **Cultural Significance:** Represents the convergence of Indigenous corn traditions, Spanish eggs, and African frying techniques. A living piece of Caribbean culinary history

‚úì **Accessibility:** Sold on virtually every street corner. Typically $1 to $2 per arepa

‚úì **Value:** A complete breakfast for under $3 is one of the best deals in the Americas

**Where We Loved It Most:** The legendary street vendors in Plaza de San Diego (particularly the late night stall that Anthony Bourdain made famous), the morning carts near Parque Fern√°ndez Madrid, and the family run stands in Chambac√∫ during the Festival del Frito.

**Verdict:** The arepa de huevo is a masterclass in street food engineering. Watching a skilled vendor execute the double fry technique ‚Äî slicing the puffy arepa, slipping in the egg, and returning it to the oil with practiced precision ‚Äî is culinary theater. Pair it with a strong cup of Colombian coffee and a squeeze of lime hot sauce. You haven't really been to Cartagena until you've had an arepa de huevo fresh from the oil at a street corner stall.

**Verdict:** Do not overlook the coconut rice. What appears to be a simple side dish is actually a carefully crafted component that took centuries to perfect. ## üç¨ Cocadas ‚Äî Best Sweet Treat

**Total Score: 87.2 / 100**

The Portal de los Dulces ‚Äî a historic arcade in Plaza de los Coches ‚Äî is ground zero for Cartagena's legendary candy culture. Here, vendors sell handmade cocadas in dozens of varieties: plain coconut, coconut with pineapple, coconut with guava, coconut with arequipe (dulce de leche), and many more.

These traditional coconut candies represent generations of candy making tradition, primarily kept alive by Palenquera women whose ancestors brought sweet making traditions from Africa. The cocadas vary from soft and chewy to firm and crunchy, but all deliver intense coconut flavor balanced with sweetness.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

‚úì **Flavor Complexity:** Intense coconut flavor balanced with sugar and often combined with tropical fruits or caramel

‚úì **Ingredient Quality:** The best cocadas use freshly grated coconut and real panela. The difference is noticeable

‚úì **Cultural Significance:** A living tradition maintained by Afro Colombian communities, particularly Palenquera women from San Basilio de Palenque

‚úì **Accessibility:** Sold throughout the Portal de los Dulces and by vendors throughout the city. Typically $0.25 to $1 per piece

‚úì **Value:** An assortment of cocadas costs just a few dollars and makes a perfect gift to bring home

**Where We Loved It Most:** The family run stands in the Portal de los Dulces (where recipes have been passed down for generations), the Palenquera vendors in colorful dress throughout the Walled City, and the Festival de los Dulces when the plaza transforms into a candy carnival.

**Verdict:** The Portal de los Dulces is a time capsule of Caribbean candy culture. Walking through the arcade, sampling cocadas from different vendors, watching the display cases full of colorful treats ‚Äî it is a sweet tooth's paradise. Try the cocada with guava for a fruity version, or the one with arequipe for pure caramel indulgence. Buy a box to take home. These simple coconut candies represent centuries of Afro Colombian tradition and taste like history.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Food in Cartagena

**1. What is the single best food to eat in Cartagena?**
Cazuela de mariscos is the definitive Cartagena food experience. Scored 95.2/100 in our evaluation, it is the city's most iconic dish and the perfect representation of Caribbean Colombian cuisine ‚Äî fresh seafood swimming in a rich coconut milk broth. Every visitor should try it at a traditional restaurant.

**2. Is Cartagena street food safe to eat?**
Yes. Cartagena street food culture is deeply established and generally safe. Stick to busy stalls with high turnover ‚Äî this ensures fresh oil and freshly cooked food. The arepa de huevo, empanadas, and carima√±olas are all widely consumed by locals daily. Watch for lines of locals as a sign of quality and freshness.

**3. How much does a meal in Cartagena cost?**
Cartagena is moderately priced by international standards but more expensive than much of Colombia. An arepa de huevo costs $1 to $2, a full plate of fried fish with sides runs $8 to $15, ceviche is typically $8 to $18, and a cazuela de mariscos ranges from $12 to $30 depending on the restaurant. A full day of excellent eating can cost as little as $25 to $40.

**4. What makes Cartagena cuisine different from other Colombian food?**
Cartagena cuisine is distinctly Caribbean, heavily influenced by African cooking traditions brought by enslaved peoples, the abundant seafood of the coast, and the widespread use of coconut in everything from rice to stews to desserts. It is sweeter, more seafood focused, and uses different spices than the highland cuisines of Bogot√° or Medell√≠n.

**5. Where is the best ceviche in Cartagena?**
La Cevicher√≠a in Plaza de San Diego remains the most famous after Anthony Bourdain's visit, and it still delivers excellent quality. Buena Vida Marisquer√≠a offers more creative presentations. For a casual experience, try the ceviche from beach vendors at Playa Blanca or Bocagrande.

**6. What time do Cartagena restaurants serve lunch and dinner?**
Lunch typically runs from 12:00 PM to 3:00 PM and dinner service starts around 7:00 PM and runs until 10:00 PM or later. Many traditional restaurants close between lunch and dinner. Street food vendors often operate from early morning through late night. Arriving early or late can help avoid crowds at popular spots.

**7. Is it worth taking a food tour in Cartagena?**
Absolutely, especially for first time visitors. A guided food tour through Getseman√≠ or the Walled City covers multiple dishes in one outing with local context and access to places you might not find on your own. Budget roughly $50 to $80 per person for a quality tour.

**8. What is titot√© and why does it matter?**
Titot√© is the caramelized coconut solids that form when coconut milk is cooked down until the water evaporates. It gives arroz con coco its signature golden brown color, sweet nutty flavor, and distinguishes authentic Cartagena coconut rice from versions made elsewhere. Ask for rice "con titot√©" for the most traditional experience.

**9. What are patacones and why are they served with everything?**
Patacones are twice fried green plantain rounds ‚Äî smashed flat between fries for a thick, starchy, satisfying crunch. They appear alongside nearly every dish as a vehicle for scooping up sauces and as a starchy balance to seafood and meat. They are essential to the Caribbean Colombian meal.

**10. Can I eat well in Cartagena on a budget?**
Cartagena offers excellent budget eating options. An arepa de huevo for $1.50, a plate of fried fish for $10, a shrimp cocktail for $5, cocadas for under $1 ‚Äî you can eat extraordinarily well for under $25 a day by sticking to street food and traditional restaurants outside the tourist center. The Bazurto Market offers particularly good value.
