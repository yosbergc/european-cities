## Frequently Asked Questions About Food in Tel Aviv

**1. What is the single best food to eat in Tel Aviv?**
Hummus is the definitive Tel Aviv food experience. Scored 97.2/100 in our evaluation, it's the city's most iconic dish and a daily ritual for locals. Every visitor should try it at a dedicated hummusiya, ideally before noon when it's freshest.

**2. Is Tel Aviv street food safe to eat?**
Yes. Tel Aviv has high food safety standards and a deeply established street food culture. Stick to busy stalls with high turnover for the freshest food. Falafel, sabich, and shawarma stands throughout the city are safe and widely consumed by locals daily.

**3. How much does a meal in Tel Aviv cost?**

**4. What is the difference between hummus and msabbaha?**
Hummus is a smooth, fully blended chickpea purÃ©e. Msabbaha (also called masabacha) uses the same base but is served warm with whole or partially mashed chickpeas mixed in, giving it more texture. Many hummus purists consider msabbaha the superior version.

**5. Where is the best hummus in Tel Aviv?**
Abu Hassan (Ali Karavan) in Jaffa is widely considered the gold standard. Expect lines, cash only, and no menu beyond hummus, msabbaha, and ful. Shlomo & Doron in Florentin offers comparable quality with shorter waits.

**6. What time do people eat in Tel Aviv?**
Breakfast runs from 8:00 AM to noon, lunch from 12:30 PM to 3:00 PM, and dinner from 7:30 PM to 11:00 PM or later. Tel Aviv is a late city; many restaurants don't fill up until 9:00 PM. For hummus, arrive early (before 11:00 AM) to beat the crowds.

**7. Is it easy to eat vegetarian or vegan in Tel Aviv?**
Tel Aviv is one of the most vegan friendly cities in the world. Hummus, falafel, sabich (without egg), shakshuka, and most mezze dishes are naturally vegetarian. Many restaurants have dedicated vegan menus, and plant based options are widely available.

**8. What is sabich and where can I find it?**
Sabich is a pita sandwich filled with fried eggplant, hard boiled egg, hummus, tahini, Israeli salad, and amba (mango pickle sauce). It originated with Iraqi Jews and is now a Tel Aviv staple. Sabich Tchernichovsky and Sabich Frishman are the most celebrated spots.

**9. What is schug and how spicy is it?**
Schug is a Yemenite chili paste made from hot peppers, garlic, and spices. Green schug (made with green chilies and cilantro) is the most common and ranges from medium to very hot. Red schug tends to be slightly milder. A small amount adds significant heat.

**10. What should I eat on Shabbat in Tel Aviv?**
Many restaurants close Friday evening through Saturday, but Tel Aviv is more secular than other Israeli cities. Jachnun is the traditional Saturday morning dish at Yemenite spots. The Carmel Market is closed, but many cafÃ©s and restaurants in central Tel Aviv remain open.
