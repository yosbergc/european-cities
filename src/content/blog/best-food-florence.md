## FAQ

**What is a *fiaschetteria* and how does it differ from an *enoteca*?**
A *fiaschetteria* (from *fiasco*, the traditional Tuscan wine flask) is one of Florence's oldest food and drink institutions â€” a simple wine shop that over time acquired the custom of serving food alongside the wine, typically *salumi*, cheese, *crostini*, and simple hot dishes. The original *fiaschetterie* were working-class establishments where labourers drank cheap Chianti by the glass and ate whatever was available. The form survives in a handful of historic establishments and in the many *enoteche* (wine shops with food service) that have inherited the tradition. The distinction between *fiaschetteria* and *enoteca* is historical rather than currently practical â€” an *enoteca* typically has a wider wine selection and a more developed food menu, but both serve the same function of eating and drinking well at an accessible price.

**What is *schiacciata* and where do we find the best versions?**
*Schiacciata* (literally "squashed") is Tuscany's version of focaccia â€” a flatbread made from olive oil-enriched dough, pressed thin, dimpled with the fingers, generously oiled, and baked until the surface is golden and slightly crispy while the interior remains soft and yielding. The Florentine version is thinner and crisper than the Ligurian *focaccia* tradition and has a pronounced olive oil character from the quality of the Tuscan oil used. It is eaten as a snack, as an accompaniment to *salumi*, as the bread for *lampredotto* sandwiches at some carts, and in a sweet version (*schiacciata con l'uva*, made with wine grapes and sugar) in September during the grape harvest. The best versions are at serious Florentine bakeries: Forno Sartoni in Via dei Cerchi and Forno Top in Via Guelfa are among the city's most respected.

**What is *olio nuovo* and why does it matter?**
*Olio nuovo* (new oil) is the olive oil pressed in November from the Tuscan harvest â€” intensely green, slightly cloudy because it has not yet been filtered, and aggressively peppery and grassy in flavour from the fresh polyphenols that settle or oxidize over the following months. *Olio nuovo* is available only in November and December; after that the oil loses its characteristic intensity and becomes the rounder, more mellow product that most people know as Tuscan olive oil. Eating in Florence during *olio nuovo* season â€” when every *trattoria* has a bottle of this year's pressing on the table and it is poured over *ribollita*, *fettunta* (grilled bread), and soup with the excitement of a newly opened wine â€” is one of the most seasonally specific and most rewarding food experiences in the city.

**What is *Vin Santo* and how is it drunk correctly?**
*Vin Santo* (holy wine) is a Tuscan dessert wine made from *Trebbiano* and *Malvasia* grapes that have been dried on straw mats or hung from rafters for several months after harvest, concentrating their sugars, then pressed and fermented in small sealed barrels (*caratelli*) where they undergo a slow, multi-year aging process. The result is an amber-coloured wine of complex character â€” dried fruit, walnut, caramel, and a slight oxidative nuttiness â€” ranging from sweet to almost dry depending on the producer and style. It is drunk from small glasses at the end of a meal, accompanied by *cantucci* (twice-baked almond biscuits from Prato, thirty kilometres from Florence) which are dipped into the wine briefly before eating. The *cantucci* should be dry and hard before the dipping; the wine should be at cellar temperature rather than chilled.

**What is the Mercato Centrale and when should we visit?**
The Mercato Centrale is Florence's main covered market, a two-floor wrought-iron market hall built in 1874 in the San Lorenzo neighbourhood. The ground floor is the original market â€” butchers, fishmongers, cheese vendors, produce stalls, dried pasta, olive oil, *schiacciata*, and the famous food carts including Nerbone, which has been serving *ribollita*, *lampredotto*, and boiled beef sandwiches since 1872. The upper floor was converted in 2014 into a food hall with multiple restaurant stalls; it is more tourist-facing and considerably more expensive than the ground floor. For the most authentic and most rewarding Florentine market experience, spend your time on the ground floor.

**What is *bistecca alla Fiorentina* actually supposed to weigh?**
The specifications are specific: a minimum thickness of five centimetres and a minimum weight of 800 grams for the small end of the accepted range, with most Florentine *trattorie* serving cuts of 1 to 1.5 kilograms. It is priced per kilogram (usually â‚¬60â€“100/kg at serious establishments), which means it is effectively always a dish for two people â€” a full-size *bistecca* is too much for one person and too little to share between three without significant inadequacy. The convention is to order one *bistecca* for two, with a side of *fagioli all'uccelletto* and perhaps a salad, and to spend approximately â‚¬45â€“65 total for the two of you on the meat course. Anything marketed as a *bistecca alla Fiorentina* weighing less than 700 grams and priced suspiciously cheaply is almost certainly not the real thing.

**Is Florence good for vegetarians?**
Better than its meat-heavy reputation suggests, particularly for those willing to engage with the *cucina povera* tradition. *Ribollita*, *pappa al pomodoro* (bread and tomato soup), *fagioli all'uccelletto* (white beans with sage and tomato), *pasta e fagioli*, and the full range of Tuscan vegetable and legume preparations are all naturally plant-based and available at most traditional *trattorie*. The Tuscan cheese tradition â€” *Pecorino Toscano* in its various aging stages, from soft and mild to aged and sharp â€” is exceptional. The *salumi* boards, obviously, are not available, but a vegetarian eating in Florence's traditional *cucina povera* register will find more depth and variety than the city's carnivorous reputation implies.

**What is Chianti Classico and how does it differ from regular Chianti?**
*Chianti Classico* is a specific DOCG (Denominazione di Origine Controllata e Garantita) wine zone covering approximately 72,000 hectares of hills between Florence and Siena â€” the historic heartland of Chianti production, distinguished from the broader Chianti DOC appellation by stricter production rules, higher minimum Sangiovese content (at least 80%), and longer aging requirements. The *Gallo Nero* (Black Rooster) symbol on the bottle indicates *Classico* status. Within the *Classico* zone, the *Riserva* category (aged a minimum of 24 months, 3 months in bottle) and the *Gran Selezione* category (the highest tier, aged a minimum of 30 months from a single vineyard or selection) represent the quality peaks of Florentine wine. The difference between a well-made *Classico Riserva* and a generic *Chianti* is very significant and worth the additional investment.

**What are the best neighbourhoods for eating in Florence?**
The Oltrarno (literally "the other side of the Arno") â€” specifically the San Frediano neighbourhood â€” has the highest concentration of serious neighbourhood *trattorie* with authentic food and local clienteles. The Sant'Ambrogio neighbourhood, east of the Duomo, has the city's best daily market and a cluster of excellent *trattorie* and wine bars in the streets around Piazza Sant'Ambrogio. Mercato Centrale and the San Lorenzo neighbourhood around it are the best area for street food and market eating. The Centro Storico (historic centre) has excellent food available but requires more careful navigation to avoid tourist-trap restaurants, which are more concentrated here than in any other part of the city.

**What should we not order in Florence?**
Any restaurant that offers *bistecca alla Fiorentina* cooked past rare should be treated with scepticism about its other offerings. The words "traditional Florentine" on a menu in large English type, visible from the street, are a reliable warning sign. *Spaghetti bolognese* (not a Florentine dish and rarely made well in the city) and generic pizza (Florence is not a pizza city) are both reliable indicators of a tourist-facing kitchen with limited commitment to local cooking. The most reliable evasion strategy is to walk to a neighbourhood away from the Piazza della Repubblicaâ€“Duomoâ€“Uffizi tourist circuit and look for a restaurant where the day's menu is written on a board in Italian and where the clientele is primarily Florentine.
