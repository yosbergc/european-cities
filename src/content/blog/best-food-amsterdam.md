## FAQ

**What is the difference between *Jong* and *Oud Jenever*?**
*Jong Jenever* (young) is a lighter, cleaner style made primarily from grain neutral spirit with a smaller proportion of *moutwijn* (malt wine base) â€” it was developed in the 20th century to appeal to palates accustomed to vodka and is closer in character to a mild gin. *Oud Jenever* (old) uses a higher proportion of *moutwijn* and is often aged in oak barrels, giving it a malty, slightly whisky-like depth and a much more complex flavour profile. The "Jong" and "Oud" designations refer to style, not age â€” though aged Jenever (*gerijpt*) is also a separate category. For the most characterful Dutch drinking experience, order *Oud Jenever*.

**What is the *Hollandse Nieuwe* season and when does it run?**
The *Hollandse Nieuwe* season opens when the first herring of the year reaches the minimum required fat content of 16%, which typically happens in late May or June â€” the exact date varies each year depending on sea temperature and fish condition. The opening is marked by an auction at Scheveningen. The season runs through approximately July or August, after which the fish begin spawning and the fat content drops. Outside this season, regular *maatjesharing* (young, lightly cured herring) is available and excellent but does not have the same fat content or ceremonial quality as the *Nieuwe*.

**Is Amsterdam good for vegetarians?**

**What is *eetcafÃ©* culture and how does it work?**

**What are *bitterballen* and should we eat them?**
*Bitterballen* are small, round, deep-fried croquettes filled with a *ragout* of beef, veal, or occasionally mushroom, bound in a thick *bechamel*-style sauce. They are served in pairs or fours on a small plate with Dutch mustard for dipping, always at an *eetcafÃ©* or *bruin cafÃ©* alongside beer or *Jenever*. **What is Indonesian food doing in Amsterdam and why is it so good?**
The Netherlands governed the Indonesian archipelago (known as the Dutch East Indies) from the 17th century until Indonesian independence in 1949. During this period, hundreds of thousands of Dutch citizens and Indonesian migrants traveled between the two countries, and the culinary exchange was profound and lasting. The Indonesian community that settled in the Netherlands brought with them a cooking tradition â€” spiced, aromatic, coconut-and-chili-forward â€” that was both entirely different from Dutch cuisine and entirely complementary to it. The *rijsttafel* format was a Dutch colonial invention, designed to showcase the range of Indonesian regional cooking for European palates, but it has been refined over decades by Indonesian families in Amsterdam into a format that is now genuinely excellent on its own terms. The reason it is so good is institutional memory: restaurants like Sama Sebo and Tempo Doeloe have been making the same preparations for thirty or forty years.

**How do we navigate the tourist-trap restaurants in Amsterdam?**
