When it comes to culinary capitals, New York City stands alone. This is the city that perfected the foldable pizza slice, transformed the bagel into a global icon, and turned deli meats into art form. Every neighborhood tells a different food story, from the pastrami temples of the Lower East Side to the pizza parlors of Brooklyn.

From stacked sandwiches at century old delis to crispy crusted slices fresh from coal fired ovens, every dish in New York carries the weight of immigrant tradition, relentless innovation, and unapologetic flavor.

We spent over five weeks eating across all five boroughs, from the iconic counters of Manhattan to the neighborhood gems of Brooklyn, Queens, and the Bronx, tasting over 70 dishes at more than 40 restaurants, delis, bakeries, and street carts. Our goal: to help you navigate the overwhelming New York food scene and identify the dishes that are absolutely essential when you visit.

## üî¨ How We Evaluated

To build this guide, we tasted each dish at a minimum of three different locations across New York City, from tourist heavy spots in Midtown to neighborhood institutions in Brooklyn, the Lower East Side, and Queens.

Every dish was scored using a 100 point evaluation system across six core categories:

**Flavor Complexity** (25%) ‚Äî Depth, balance, and layering of taste

**Ingredient Quality** (20%) ‚Äî Freshness, sourcing, and authenticity

**Cultural Significance** (15%) ‚Äî Historical roots and local importance

**Accessibility** (15%) ‚Äî How easy it is to find a great version

**Value for Money** (15%) ‚Äî Portion quality relative to cost

**Overall Experience** (10%) ‚Äî The total sensory and emotional impact

We evaluated dishes in their most traditional preparations, at locations ranging from James Beard recognized establishments to family run spots that have been serving the same recipes for generations.

## ü•á New York Pizza Slice ‚Äî Best Overall Dish in NYC

**Total Score: 95.8 / 100**

There is no separating New York from its pizza. The classic New York slice, a thin, wide triangle of slightly charred crust with a perfect chew, tangy tomato sauce, and stretchy mozzarella, is not just a meal. It is a way of life. You fold it, you walk with it, you let the grease drip down your arm. This is how New York eats.

What makes a New York slice different from every other pizza on the planet is the combination of high gluten bread flour, low moisture mozzarella, and deck ovens that cook the pie fast and hot. The result: a crust that is crisp underneath, pliable enough to fold, with edges that achieve a slight char without being burned.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

‚úì **Flavor Complexity:** The interplay between sweet, slightly acidic tomato sauce and the browned, stretchy mozzarella creates layers of umami that build with every bite

‚úì **Ingredient Quality:** Top slice shops use proprietary dough recipes, often fermented 24 to 48 hours, and source their cheese specifically for melt and pull characteristics

‚úì **Cultural Significance:** New York style pizza evolved from Italian immigrants in the early 1900s and became the blueprint for American pizza culture

‚úì **Accessibility:** Available at over 1,500 pizzerias across the five boroughs. Prices range from $3.50 to $6 per slice at quality establishments

‚úì **Value:** A full meal for under $8 including a drink remains one of the best deals in the city

**Where We Loved It Most:** L'Industrie Pizzeria in Williamsburg (imported Italian ingredients, burrata slice is legendary), Joe's Pizza in the West Village (the benchmark for classic New York slice since 1975), and Scarr's Pizza on the Lower East Side (custom milled flour, natural fermentation, an evolution of the form).

**Verdict:** If you eat only one thing in New York, this is it. The New York pizza slice is more than food. It is a ritual, a cultural artifact, and a reminder that simplicity, executed with precision, produces greatness. Every slice shop we tried delivered something special, but the best versions will recalibrate your understanding of what pizza can be.

## ü•à Pastrami Sandwich ‚Äî Best Deli Experience

**Total Score: 94.2 / 100**

The pastrami sandwich is New York's contribution to world sandwich culture. A towering stack of hand sliced, peppery, smoke kissed beef brisket piled onto fresh seeded rye bread with nothing but spicy brown mustard. It is impossibly tender, intensely flavored, and unapologetically indulgent.

The process behind great pastrami is what separates the legends from the pretenders. The beef is cured in brine for up to three weeks, coated in a secret blend of spices, slow smoked for days, then steamed until it practically melts. Hand slicing adds an additional layer of care that machine cutting cannot replicate.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

‚úì **Flavor Complexity:** Layers of smoke, pepper, coriander, and beef fat create a flavor profile that is simultaneously bold and nuanced

‚úì **Ingredient Quality:** Top delis cure and smoke their own meat in house, a process that can take over a month from start to finish

‚úì **Cultural Significance:** Arrived on the Lower East Side with Romanian Jewish immigrants in the late 1800s. Katz's has been serving it since 1888

‚úì **Accessibility:** Available at Jewish delis across the city, though the legendary spots are concentrated on the Lower East Side and Upper West Side. Typically $25 to $32 per sandwich

‚úì **Value:** Sandwiches are massive, often split between two people. You are paying for a month of preparation in every bite

**Where We Loved It Most:** Katz's Delicatessen on the Lower East Side (the gold standard, hand carved to order since 1888), Pastrami Queen on the Upper West Side (machine sliced but exceptionally smoky, served on outstanding rye from Orwasher's), and 2nd Avenue Deli (the tissue thin slices practically dissolve on the tongue).

**Verdict:** A properly made pastrami sandwich is one of the great eating experiences in America. The combination of tender, fatty meat, sharp mustard, and soft rye bread is deceptively simple but requires decades of accumulated expertise to execute perfectly. Skip the turkey. Order the pastrami.

## ü•â Bagel with Lox ‚Äî Best Breakfast Experience

**Total Score: 93.5 / 100**

The bagel and lox is New York's definitive breakfast. A hand rolled, kettle boiled bagel, sliced and spread with cream cheese, then topped with silky cured salmon, capers, thinly sliced red onion, and sometimes tomato. It is a textural masterpiece: the chewy, slightly sweet bagel against the buttery, briny fish.

What makes a New York bagel different is the water (allegedly), the boiling process that creates the shiny crust, and the hand rolling technique that gives the interior its signature dense, chewy crumb. No other city has replicated it successfully, though many have tried.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

‚úì **Flavor Complexity:** The salted, silky lox against tangy cream cheese, with the sharp bite of capers and raw onion, creates a symphony of flavors that defines New York breakfast culture

‚úì **Ingredient Quality:** The best shops hand roll and kettle boil their bagels daily. Lox quality varies wildly; top spots cure and slice their own

‚úì **Cultural Significance:** Brought to New York by Eastern European Jewish immigrants, the bagel and lox became the city's dominant breakfast by the mid 20th century

‚úì **Accessibility:** Available at bagel shops and appetizing stores across all five boroughs. A loaded bagel runs $15 to $25 at premium spots

‚úì **Value:** Premium pricing but premium experience. This is not gas station bagel territory

**Where We Loved It Most:** Russ & Daughters on East Houston Street (the 111 year old appetizing shop sets the standard for smoked fish and the full classic experience), Ess a Bagel in Midtown (the bagels themselves are exceptional, oversized with perfect chew), and Tompkins Square Bagels in the East Village (innovative flavors on a traditional foundation).

**Verdict:** The bagel and lox is more than breakfast. It is a cultural institution, a connection to the immigrant communities that built this city, and one of the few truly non negotiable food experiences in New York. Stand at the counter. Take a number. Get the classic.

## üç∞ New York Cheesecake ‚Äî Best Dessert Experience

**Total Score: 91.7 / 100**

New York cheesecake is dense, creamy, and unapologetically rich. Unlike lighter European styles, the New York version uses heavy cream and sour cream to create a velvety texture that holds its shape when sliced but melts on the tongue. Typically baked on a thin sponge cake or graham cracker crust, it requires nothing but a fork to appreciate fully.

The key to New York cheesecake is restraint. No fruit swirls, no chocolate drizzle, no distractions. Just cream cheese, eggs, sugar, and a mastery of temperature control during baking to prevent cracks and achieve the signature smooth top.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

‚úì **Flavor Complexity:** The tanginess of cream cheese balanced against sweetness, with vanilla and lemon undertones that reveal themselves slowly

‚úì **Ingredient Quality:** Premium cream cheese is non negotiable. The best versions use fresh heavy cream and bake in small batches

‚úì **Cultural Significance:** Popularized by Jewish delis in the 1920s and 1930s, New York cheesecake became synonymous with celebration and special occasions

‚úì **Accessibility:** Available at bakeries, diners, and restaurants citywide. A slice typically runs $8 to $14

‚úì **Value:** Slices are substantial. One piece is dessert for two unless you are committed

**Where We Loved It Most:** Junior's in Brooklyn (the original recipe since 1950, rated best in the city by New York Magazine in a blind taste test), Eileen's Special Cheesecake in SoHo (smaller, personal sized portions in multiple flavors), and Veniero's in the East Village (Italian style with incredible smoothness).

**Verdict:** New York cheesecake is not subtle. It is rich, decadent, and absolutely unapologetic about being a celebration of dairy. One slice will end your meal definitively. That is exactly the point.

## üå≠ Street Cart Hot Dog ‚Äî Best Street Food

**Total Score: 89.4 / 100**

The New York street hot dog is an icon. An all beef frankfurter with natural casing that snaps when you bite through, nestled in a soft steamed bun, topped with spicy brown mustard, sauerkraut, and sweet onion relish. Purchased from a metal cart on a Manhattan sidewalk for a few dollars. It is fast, it is portable, and it is deeply satisfying.

What elevates the street cart hot dog beyond other cities is the ubiquity and consistency. These carts run all day, the turnover is constant, and the frankfurters have that distinctive New York snap that comes from natural casing and proper griddling.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

‚úì **Flavor Complexity:** The snappy, beefy frankfurter against tangy sauerkraut and sharp mustard creates a fast, complete flavor experience

‚úì **Ingredient Quality:** Most carts use Sabrett or Nathan's all beef franks. Quality varies by cart, but high turnover spots serve them fresh

‚úì **Cultural Significance:** Street hot dogs have been a New York institution since the early 1900s. Nathan's Famous opened in Coney Island in 1916

‚úì **Accessibility:** Carts on virtually every busy corner in Manhattan. Typically $3 to $5 per dog

‚úì **Value:** A full meal for under $6. One of the last true cheap eats in the city

**Where We Loved It Most:** Gray's Papaya on the Upper West Side (the Recession Special of two dogs and a drink is legendary), Katz's Deli hot dog counter (kosher all beef franks, no waiting in the pastrami line), and the high turnover carts outside Central Park and Rockefeller Center for the authentic street experience.

**Verdict:** A street hot dog is not gourmet food. It is not supposed to be. It is fuel, it is nostalgia, it is New York in edible form. Get one with mustard and sauerkraut. Eat it standing on the sidewalk. That is the experience.

## ü•ê Cronut ‚Äî Best Pastry Innovation

**Total Score: 88.9 / 100**

The Cronut is a trademarked creation of Chef Dominique Ansel that launched in 2013 and sparked a global pastry phenomenon. A croissant doughnut hybrid made from laminated dough, proofed, fried in grapeseed oil, filled with flavored cream, and topped with glaze. It is flaky, it is rich, and each month brings a new flavor that never repeats.

The process takes three days: the laminated dough is folded and rested multiple times to create hundreds of layers, then shaped, proofed, fried, filled, and glazed by hand. The result is unlike anything else in the pastry world.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

‚úì **Flavor Complexity:** The layered, buttery dough against sweet cream filling and flavored glaze creates textural contrasts that evolve with every bite

‚úì **Ingredient Quality:** Made fresh daily in small batches using proprietary techniques. Each Cronut represents days of labor

‚úì **Cultural Significance:** The Cronut became the most viral dessert in food history, spawning global imitators and establishing Dominique Ansel as a pastry innovator

‚úì **Accessibility:** Available only at Dominique Ansel Bakery in SoHo or via limited pre order. Lines form before opening. Limit 2 per person. Approximately $8 each

‚úì **Value:** Premium pricing for a premium, one of a kind product. Worth it at least once

**Where We Loved It Most:** Dominique Ansel Bakery in SoHo is the only place to get the authentic Cronut. Accept no substitutes or imitators.

**Verdict:** The Cronut is a bucket list item for serious food enthusiasts. Is it essential to the New York food experience? Perhaps not. But it represents the city's spirit of innovation and the idea that even pastry can be reinvented. Arrive early. Be patient. It rewards the effort.

## ü•™ Bodega Bacon Egg and Cheese ‚Äî Best Grab and Go

**Total Score: 88.2 / 100**

The bodega bacon egg and cheese is the unofficial breakfast of working New York. A fried egg, crispy bacon, and melted American cheese on a fresh roll, wrapped in foil and handed across a deli counter while you grab coffee. It is cheap, it is fast, and it tastes like New York mornings.

The magic is in the simplicity and the variations. Salt pepper ketchup? Avocado? Hot sauce? Every New Yorker has their order memorized. The egg is cooked to order, the bacon is crispy, and the cheese melts into everything, holding it all together.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

‚úì **Flavor Complexity:** The runny yolk mixing with salty bacon and gooey cheese creates comfort food perfection

‚úì **Ingredient Quality:** Varies by bodega, but high volume spots with constant turnover produce the freshest versions

‚úì **Cultural Significance:** The BEC is the great equalizer. Wall Street bankers and construction workers stand in the same line at 7am

‚úì **Accessibility:** Available at corner bodegas and delis across all five boroughs. Typically $5 to $8

‚úì **Value:** A full, satisfying breakfast for the price of a fancy coffee

**Where We Loved It Most:** Any high volume bodega in Midtown during morning rush, Sunny and Annie's Deli in the East Village (cult following for good reason), and virtually any corner store where you see workers lined up at 7am.

**Verdict:** The bacon egg and cheese is not a destination dish. It is a way of life. Ordering one correctly (saltpepperketchup said as one word) marks you as someone who understands New York. Get one. Eat it while walking. Join the city.

## üçù Italian American Red Sauce ‚Äî Best Sit Down Dinner

**Total Score: 87.6 / 100**

The red sauce joint is a New York institution. Checkered tablecloths, bread baskets with garlic knots, and massive portions of spaghetti and meatballs, chicken parmigiana, and baked ziti swimming in tomato sauce and melted mozzarella. This is not refined Italian cuisine. It is Italian American abundance.

These restaurants trace their roots to the Italian immigrant communities of the early 1900s, who adapted peasant recipes to American ingredients and American appetites. The result is a cuisine that exists nowhere else, not even Italy.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

‚úì **Flavor Complexity:** Long simmered tomato sauces with garlic, basil, and olive oil provide deep, comforting flavor

‚úì **Ingredient Quality:** Classic spots make their own pasta, sauce, and meatballs. Cheese is applied generously

‚úì **Cultural Significance:** Italian American cuisine emerged from New York's immigrant neighborhoods and influenced American food culture more than almost any other tradition

‚úì **Accessibility:** Red sauce joints exist in every borough, with concentrations in Little Italy, Carroll Gardens, and Arthur Avenue. Entrees typically $18 to $35

‚úì **Value:** Portions are enormous. Taking home leftovers is expected and encouraged

**Where We Loved It Most:** Rao's in East Harlem (if you can get a table; near impossible reservations but legendary for reason), John's of 12th Street in the East Village (old school ambiance, excellent veal parmigiana), and Bamonte's in Williamsburg (operating since 1900, unchanged and perfect).

**Verdict:** A meal at a proper red sauce joint is a step back in time. The pace is slow, the portions are huge, and the experience is distinctly New York. Come hungry. Leave with leftovers.

## üß† Final Verdict: What Should You Eat in New York?

Every dish on this list earned its spot through weeks of on the ground tasting. But your priorities and appetite will determine what you chase first. Here is our scenario based breakdown:

**First time in New York?** Head to Joe's Pizza or L'Industrie. A New York slice is the single most important food experience in the city and the benchmark for American pizza culture.

**Morning ritual?** Stand in line at Russ & Daughters or Ess a Bagel. Get the classic lox, cream cheese, capers, and onion on an everything bagel. Accept no substitutes.

**Quick lunch under $10?** Grab a street hot dog with mustard and sauerkraut from a high volume cart, or duck into any bodega for a bacon egg and cheese.

**Destination meal?** Katz's Deli for pastrami is non negotiable. Take a number, tip the cutter, and share a sandwich. The pickles are free.

**Special occasion dessert?** Junior's cheesecake in Brooklyn or the Times Square location. The original plain is the move.

**Foodie bucket list?** The Cronut at Dominique Ansel requires planning and patience, but represents New York's spirit of culinary innovation.

**Old school dinner?** Find a red sauce joint with checkered tablecloths and order the chicken parm or spaghetti and meatballs. Leave with leftovers.

No matter what you choose, every dish on this list represents New York food culture at its most authentic. This is not a city that does things halfway. The portions are big, the flavors are bold, and the history runs deep. Those traditions, as we discovered firsthand, are more than worth the trip.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Food in New York

**1. What is the single best food to eat in New York City?**

The New York pizza slice is the definitive NYC food experience. Scored 95.8/100 in our evaluation, it represents the city's Italian immigrant heritage and has influenced pizza culture worldwide. Every visitor should try one at a classic slice shop.

**2. How much does a meal in New York cost?**

New York offers food at every price point. A pizza slice costs $3.50 to $6, a street hot dog runs $3 to $5, and a bacon egg and cheese is $5 to $8. At the premium end, a pastrami sandwich costs $25 to $32, and a sit down dinner can range from $18 to $50 per person depending on the restaurant.

**3. Where is the best pizza in New York City?**

L'Industrie Pizzeria in Williamsburg offers the best combination of quality ingredients and innovative toppings. Joe's Pizza in the West Village is the benchmark for classic New York style. Scarr's Pizza on the Lower East Side represents the evolution of the form with custom milled flour and natural fermentation.

**4. Is it safe to eat street food in New York?**

Yes. Street vendors are licensed and inspected by the New York City Department of Health. Carts receive letter grades just like restaurants. Look for high turnover carts with long lines, which ensures fresh, hot food.

**5. What is the best deli in New York?**

Katz's Delicatessen has been operating since 1888 and serves the most iconic pastrami sandwich in the city. Pastrami Queen on the Upper West Side is excellent for those who prefer machine sliced meat on superior rye bread. 2nd Avenue Deli offers a more intimate, traditional experience.

**6. Where should I eat breakfast in NYC?**

For the classic bagel and lox experience, Russ & Daughters on the Lower East Side is unmatched. For oversized, perfectly chewy bagels, Ess a Bagel in Midtown is excellent. For a quick, cheap option, any high volume bodega serves solid bacon egg and cheese sandwiches.

**7. What time do New Yorkers eat lunch and dinner?**

Lunch typically runs from 12:00 PM to 2:00 PM, and dinner service starts around 6:00 PM and runs until 10:00 PM or later at popular spots. Many delis and pizza shops serve continuously throughout the day with no break.

**8. Is the Cronut worth the hype?**

The Cronut at Dominique Ansel Bakery is a bucket list pastry experience, but it requires planning. Pre orders open Mondays at 11am EST, or arrive before the 8am opening for the daily batch. It is an innovative, delicious creation, but not essential to the core New York food experience.

**9. What is a bodega and why should I visit one?**

A bodega is a corner convenience store unique to New York City, typically open 24 hours and selling everything from groceries to freshly made sandwiches. The bacon egg and cheese sandwich is the signature bodega item and represents authentic, everyday New York eating.

**10. Can I eat well in New York on a budget?**

New York is one of the best budget food cities in America if you know where to look. A pizza slice for $4, a hot dog for $4, a BEC for $6, and a cheesecake slice for $8 means you can eat extraordinarily well for under $25 a day without sacrificing quality or authenticity.
