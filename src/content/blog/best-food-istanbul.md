Istanbul does not merely sit at the crossroads of two continents  it tastes like it. The city's food culture is the most layered in Europe or Asia, the product of a civilization that ruled from the Balkans to the Arabian Peninsula for six centuries and organized its kitchens with the same imperial ambition it brought to architecture and law. Ottoman palace cooking  the *TopkapÄ±* kitchen that fed thousands of courtiers daily and developed the techniques that would spread through the Mediterranean, the Middle East, and the Balkans  is the invisible backbone of what you eat in Istanbul today. You taste it in the patience of a slow-braised lamb, in the precision with which pomegranate molasses balances a walnut filling, in the conviction that butter, spice, and time are the three most important ingredients in any serious kitchen. Istanbul did not inherit a cuisine. It built one over eight hundred years, and it has been refining it ever since.

What makes eating in Istanbul uniquely overwhelming is the sheer density and range of the experience.
