## ü•á Asado (Parrillada) ‚Äî Best Overall Dish in Buenos Aires

**Total Score: 97.2 / 100**

There is no separating Buenos Aires from its beef. Asado is not merely a meal in Argentina. It is a sacred social ritual, a Sunday institution, and the purest expression of the country's culinary identity. A proper parrillada delivers an orchestrated procession of grilled meats: chorizo and morcilla (blood sausage) first, then achuras (offal) like mollejas (sweetbreads), followed by the main event of thick cut steaks cooked over wood or charcoal fire.

What makes Argentine asado different from any other grilled meat on the planet is the combination of grass fed beef from the pampas, the slow cooking technique over fuego lento (low fire), and cuts you simply cannot find elsewhere. The bife de chorizo (sirloin), entra√±a (skirt steak), vac√≠o (flank), and tira de asado (short ribs) each offer distinct textures and flavor profiles.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

**Flavor Complexity:** The interplay between the smoky char, the naturally sweet grass fed beef, and the punch of chimichurri creates a depth that borders on transcendent

**Ingredient Quality:** Top parrillas source from specific ranches. Don Julio ages cuts in house for up to 28 days. The difference from commercial beef is unmistakable

**Cultural Significance:** Asado predates Argentina as a nation. It is tied to gaucho culture, family gatherings, and national identity itself

**Accessibility:** Available at hundreds of parrillas across the city. Prices range from $15 to $80+ per person depending on the establishment

**Value:** Even at world renowned spots, you receive portions that could feed two

**Where We Loved It Most:** Don Julio in Palermo (ranked No. 1 on the World's Best Steak Restaurants list, one Michelin star), La Cabrera for its legendary side dishes and premium cuts, and Hierro Parrilla in San Telmo Market for outstanding quality at accessible prices.

**Verdict:** If you eat only one thing in Buenos Aires, it must be asado at a proper parrilla. Don Julio remains the gold standard, but the beauty of Buenos Aires is that excellent grilled meat exists on nearly every corner. The ritual of sitting down to a long meal with wine, provoleta, and multiple cuts of beef is the defining Buenos Aires food experience.

## ü•à Empanadas ‚Äî Best Handheld Food

**Total Score: 93.8 / 100**

Empanadas are Argentina's great unifier. Every province claims its own style: Salta's are small, spicy, and baked in clay ovens. Tucum√°n's are juicy with hand cut beef. Buenos Aires collects them all and adds its own spin. These small pastries, filled with seasoned meat, cheese, corn, or vegetables and either baked or fried, are the country's ultimate grab and go food.

A properly made empanada should have a repulgue (crimp) so distinctive you can identify the filling without opening it. The dough should be thin enough to taste the filling but sturdy enough to hold together. The filling must be juicy without being soggy.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

**Ingredient Quality:** La Cocina's Catamarca style empanadas use a slightly sweet butter dough that sets them apart. The carne cortada a cuchillo (hand cut beef) versions are vastly superior to ground meat

**Cultural Significance:** Empanadas criollas were declared Cultural Heritage by the Argentine Ministry of Culture. They are served at every celebration, from birthdays to national holidays

**Accessibility:** Sold at dedicated empanaderas, bakeries, parrillas, and delivery spots across every neighborhood. Typically $1.50 to $3 each

**Value:** A dozen empanadas for $20 to $30 is one of the best food deals in the city

**Where We Loved It Most:** La Cocina in Recoleta (since 1977, try the legendary Pikachu with spicy cheese and onion), Cuman√° for regional variety in a nostalgic setting, and El Sanjuanino for authentic San Juan style with live folk music.

**Verdict:** Do not leave Buenos Aires without trying empanadas from multiple sources. The fried versions offer shattering crusts while baked versions let the filling shine. La Cocina's 40+ year reputation is well earned, and the Pikachu empanada alone is worth crossing the city for.

Buenos Aires pizza is nothing like what you know. Forget thin Neapolitan crusts. Porte√±o pizza is thick, spongy, and buried under obscene amounts of mozzarella. But the crown jewel of Argentine pizza is the fugazzeta: a double crusted pizza stuffed with cheese, topped with slow cooked onions, and invented right here in the La Boca neighborhood.

The fugazzeta was created by Juan Banchero sometime between 1893 and 1932. He sliced a fugazza (onion pizza without cheese) in half and added mozzarella so it would not be "so dry." The result became an icon of Porte√±o gastronomy.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

**Ingredient Quality:** The best versions use fresh mozzarella that stretches in long strings. La Mezzetta reportedly uses about 1.5 kilos of cheese per pizza

**Cultural Significance:** Pizzer√≠a Banchero was declared a Site of Cultural Interest by the City Government in 2002. The fugazzeta is as Buenos Aires as tango

**Accessibility:** Available at pizzerias across the city. A full pizza costs $8 to $15

**Value:** One fugazzeta easily feeds two people. Adding fain√° (chickpea flatbread) on top is the local move

**Where We Loved It Most:** Pizzer√≠a Banchero in La Boca (the birthplace), La Mezzetta in Villa Ort√∫zar (standing room only, four varieties, no compromises), and G√ºerr√≠n on Avenida Corrientes (the most famous pizzeria in Argentina).

## üçñ Milanesa ‚Äî Best Comfort Dish

**Total Score: 91.4 / 100**

Often called Argentina's unofficial national dish, the milanesa is a thin slice of beef (or chicken) pounded flat, breaded, and fried until golden and crunchy. It traces back to the Italian cotoletta alla milanese but has evolved into something deeply Argentine.

The milanesa napolitana takes it further: topped with tomato sauce, ham, and melted mozzarella. It is comfort food elevated to absurd, plate covering proportions. The best milanesas curl at the edges from the heat, stay tender inside despite the crunch, and arrive accompanied by mountains of french fries.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

**Flavor Complexity:** The contrast between shattering breadcrumb exterior and juicy meat interior is addictive. A squeeze of lemon ties everything together

**Ingredient Quality:** El Preferido de Palermo uses bife de chorizo from regeneratively raised cattle, breadcrumbs from house made bread, and farm eggs

**Cultural Significance:** Every Argentine family has their own milanesa recipe. It is the food of childhood, weeknight dinners, and late night cravings

**Accessibility:** Available at bodegones, cantinas, and dedicated milanesa restaurants across the city. Typically $8 to $15

**Value:** Portions are famously enormous. El Ferroviario's milanesa serves four people easily

**Where We Loved It Most:** El Preferido de Palermo (the sirloin milanesa is perfection), Don Ignacio in Almagro (rock and roll themed shrine to the dish), and Mi T√≠o in San Telmo (the chicken version with unreal crunch).

**Verdict:** The milanesa represents Argentina's mastery of simple ingredients done exceptionally well. Whether you prefer it classic with lemon and fries or napolitana style with all the toppings, it is an essential Buenos Aires experience. Come hungry because the portions are not designed for moderation.

## üç™ Alfajores ‚Äî Best Sweet Treat

**Total Score: 89.7 / 100**

The alfajor is Argentina's cookie, and dulce de leche is its soul. Two crumbly, tender cookies sandwiching thick dulce de leche, coated in chocolate or dusted with powdered sugar or rolled in shredded coconut. It is consumed at breakfast with coffee, as an afternoon snack, and brought home by the box as gifts.

The recipe arrived with Spanish immigrants and evolved into something uniquely Argentine. Today, regional variations abound, but the Buenos Aires style prioritizes thick dulce de leche and high quality chocolate coating.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

**Flavor Complexity:** The interplay between crumbly cookie, sticky sweet dulce de leche, and bitter chocolate creates a perfect balance

**Ingredient Quality:** Havanna and Cachafaz use premium chocolate and proprietary dulce de leche recipes. The cookies should melt, not crumble

**Cultural Significance:** Alfajores are given as gifts, eaten at celebrations, and represent Argentine hospitality. They are in every kiosk, bakery, and supermarket

**Accessibility:** Available everywhere from corner kiosks to specialty shops. Typically $1 to $4 each depending on brand and size

**Value:** A box of 12 premium Havanna alfajores runs about $20 and makes the perfect souvenir

**Where We Loved It Most:** Havanna (the gold standard since Mar del Plata), Cachafaz (founded by a former Havanna owner, arguably superior chocolate coating), and local bakeries throughout Palermo for artisanal versions.

**Verdict:** You cannot visit Buenos Aires without eating multiple alfajores. The chocolate covered versions from Havanna remain the benchmark, but seeking out artisanal versions at local bakeries reveals the full spectrum of what this treat can be. Bring an extra suitcase for the boxes you will want to take home.

## üßÄ Provoleta ‚Äî Best Appetizer

**Total Score: 88.9 / 100**

Provoleta is the opening act at any serious asado. A thick disc of provolone cheese, placed directly on the grill until the bottom caramelizes into a golden crust while the top melts into stretchy, smoky perfection. It arrives still bubbling, drizzled with olive oil and oregano.

The dish exists because of Italian immigration and the Argentine obsession with grilling everything. It is simple, it is decadent, and it is essential.

**Key Tasting Highlights:**

**Flavor Complexity:** Smoky, salty, and rich with a caramelized crust that adds textural contrast to the molten interior

**Ingredient Quality:** The cheese must be thick enough to form a proper crust without melting entirely. Quality oregano and olive oil finish the dish

**Cultural Significance:** Provoleta is the official start to any asado. Eating it while waiting for the meat is the correct ritual

**Accessibility:** Available at every parrilla in the city. Typically $6 to $12

**Value:** Easily shareable as a table starter before the main event

**Where We Loved It Most:** Don Julio (multiple provoleta variations on the menu), La Cabrera (served alongside their famous endless side dishes), and El Preferido de Palermo (the provoleta with fain√° combination is exceptional).

**Verdict:** Never skip the provoleta. It sets the tone for the meal, satisfies hunger while waiting for meat, and showcases the magic of Argentine fire cooking. The caramelized bottom is the reward for patience.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Food in Buenos Aires

**1. What is the single best food to eat in Buenos Aires?**

Asado at a proper parrilla is the definitive Buenos Aires food experience. Scored 97.2/100 in our evaluation, it represents the city's cultural identity and culinary excellence. Every visitor should experience a long lunch or dinner at a traditional steakhouse.

**2. Is Buenos Aires street food safe to eat?**

Yes. Buenos Aires street food culture is well established, particularly at high turnover locations like the Costanera stands, San Telmo Market, and busy empanada counters. Stick to vendors with long lines and visible grilling, and you will eat safely and deliciously.

**3. How much does a meal in Buenos Aires cost?**

Buenos Aires offers exceptional value compared to major food cities. A parrilla meal with wine runs $25 to $60 per person. Pizza and a beer costs $10 to $15. Street food like chorip√°n and empanadas can keep you fed for under $10 per day. Even world class establishments like Don Julio remain accessible compared to comparable restaurants globally.

**4. What is the difference between fugazza and fugazzeta?**

Fugazza is a pizza topped only with onions, no cheese, no tomato. It derives from the Genoese focaccia. Fugazzeta takes it further: two layers of dough stuffed with mozzarella and topped with onions. The fugazzeta was invented at Pizzer√≠a Banchero in La Boca.

**5. Where is the best steak in Buenos Aires?**

Don Julio in Palermo consistently ranks as the best steakhouse in the world. It holds one Michelin star and tops the World's Best Steak Restaurants list. Other excellent options include La Cabrera for its famous side dishes, Fog√≥n Asado for an interactive experience, and Hierro Parrilla for quality at accessible prices.

**6. What time do Argentines eat lunch and dinner?**

Lunch typically runs from 1:00 PM to 3:30 PM. Dinner service starts around 8:30 or 9:00 PM and runs past midnight. Arriving early (before 1:30 PM for lunch or 8:30 PM for dinner) helps avoid long waits at popular spots. Many restaurants close between lunch and dinner.

**7. Is it worth taking a food tour in Buenos Aires?**

Absolutely, especially for first time visitors. A guided food tour through San Telmo, Palermo, or La Boca covers multiple dishes with local context and access to places you might not discover on your own. Budget approximately $50 to $100 per person for a quality tour.

**8. What are the best neighborhoods for food in Buenos Aires?**

Palermo offers the highest concentration of excellent restaurants, from traditional parrillas to modern gastronomy. San Telmo has the historic market and classic bodegas. Recoleta features elegant caf√©s and traditional spots. Villa Crespo has emerged as the hub for contemporary dining. La Boca is essential for pizza history.

**9. What is chimichurri and where can I buy it?**

Chimichurri is a green sauce made from parsley, oregano, garlic, vinegar, olive oil, and spices. It accompanies nearly every grilled meat dish in Argentina. You can buy jars at any supermarket, but the best versions are made fresh at parrillas. Salsa criolla (onion, bell pepper, tomato, vinegar) is the other essential accompaniment.

**10. Can I eat well in Buenos Aires on a budget?**

Buenos Aires may be the best value food city in the Americas. A slice of pizza and a portion of fain√° runs $4 to $6. A dozen empanadas costs $20 to $30. A chorip√°n from a street stand is under $5. Bakery pastries and alfajores provide cheap breakfast options. You can eat extraordinarily well for under $30 per day.
