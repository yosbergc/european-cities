## Why Trust This Bali Food Guide?

Most "best food in Bali" lists are written by people who ate at three tourist restaurants in Seminyak and called it research.

We took a different approach.

Over three weeks, our team visited 40+ warungs, night markets, and restaurants across six regions of Bali. We ordered the same core dishes at multiple locations, photographed every plate, recorded prices in Indonesian Rupiah, and scored each dish on a weighted scale covering flavor complexity, ingredient quality, texture, cultural authenticity, and value.

The result is the most data-backed Bali food guide available online. Every dish below earned its spot through repeat testing, not Instagram hype.

**Regions covered:** Ubud, Seminyak, Canggu, Denpasar, Sanur, Jimbaran, Nusa Dua, Gianyar, Klungkung

**Total dishes evaluated:** 87

**Dishes that made the final list:** 12

## How We Scored Each Dish

Each dish was evaluated using a five-category scoring system. This is the same approach we use across all our food guides to ensure consistency and fairness.

| Category | Weight | What We Measured |
|---|---|---|
| Flavor Complexity | 30% | Depth of spice layers, balance of sweet/savory/heat, aromatic quality |
| Ingredient Quality | 20% | Freshness of proteins, quality of rice, sambal preparation |
| Texture | 15% | Crunch, tenderness, contrast between components on the plate |
| Cultural Authenticity | 20% | Adherence to traditional preparation methods, regional accuracy |
| Value | 15% | Price relative to portion size and overall quality |

**Maximum possible score: 100/100**

Scores were averaged across multiple tastings at different locations. A dish needed a minimum of 97.2/100 to earn our "Exceptional" rating.

## The 12 Best Foods in Bali (Ranked by Score)

### 1. Babi Guling (Roasted Suckling Pig)

**Overall Score: 97.8/100**

| Category | Score |
|---|---|
| Flavor Complexity | 29.4/30 |
| Ingredient Quality | 19.2/20 |
| Texture | 14.8/15 |
| Cultural Authenticity | 19.6/20 |
| Value | 14.8/15 |

Babi Guling is the undisputed king of Balinese cuisine. A whole pig is stuffed with a spice paste called basa genep, which typically includes turmeric, coriander, lemongrass, black pepper, garlic, and galangal. The pig is then spit roasted over an open flame for several hours until the meat turns impossibly tender and the skin shatters like glass at the first bite.

A standard plate comes with steamed rice, sliced pork, crispy skin, lawar (a vegetable and coconut side dish), pork sausage (urutan), a spicy sambal, and a bowl of pork broth. The contrast between the shattering crackling, the silky meat, and the fiery sambal makes this easily the most complex single plate of food on the island.

**Where we tested it:** Warung Babi Guling Ibu Oka (Ubud), Babi Guling Pak Malen (Seminyak), Babi Guling Chandra (Denpasar), Warung Babi Guling Pande Egi (Gianyar)

**Best version:** Pak Malen in Seminyak delivered the crispiest skin and most balanced spice profile in our testing. The crunchy red fried pork here is exceptional. Ibu Oka remains iconic but can feel overhyped relative to price.

**Average price:** IDR 45,000 to IDR 65,000 per plate ($2.80 to $4.10 USD)

**Pro tip:** Babi Guling warungs cook a fixed number of pigs daily and close once they sell out. Arrive before 11:30 AM for the best selection.

### 2. Nasi Campur Bali (Balinese Mixed Rice)

**Overall Score: 97.2/100**

| Category | Score |
|---|---|
| Flavor Complexity | 29.0/30 |
| Ingredient Quality | 19.4/20 |
| Texture | 14.6/15 |
| Cultural Authenticity | 19.8/20 |
| Value | 14.4/15 |

Nasi Campur translates to "mixed rice," but calling it that undersells the experience by a wide margin. A proper Balinese Nasi Campur is a curated plate of steamed rice surrounded by small portions of shredded chicken or pork, sate lilit, lawar, tempeh, peanuts, a boiled egg, kerupuk (crackers), and at least one sambal. Every component is cooked separately and seasoned differently, which means each forkful delivers a completely unique flavor combination.

The Balinese version runs noticeably spicier and more turmeric heavy than Nasi Campur served elsewhere in Indonesia. The best versions use the "tunjuk" system, where you walk up to a glass display and point at whatever sides you want. The server builds your plate on the spot.

**Where we tested it:** Warung Wardani (Denpasar), Warung Men Weti (Sanur), Warung Nasi Ayam Bu Oki (Tuban), Warung Sika (Canggu)

**Best version:** Warung Wardani in Denpasar consistently assembled the most complete and well balanced plate in our testing. The shredded chicken, lawar, and sambal matah here are all made in house daily.

**Average price:** IDR 25,000 to IDR 50,000 per plate ($1.60 to $3.15 USD)

**Pro tip:** The best Nasi Campur is served at breakfast and lunch. By dinner, many warungs have run through their freshest preparations.

### 3. Sate Lilit (Balinese Minced Satay)

**Overall Score: 96.4/100**

| Category | Score |
|---|---|
| Flavor Complexity | 28.8/30 |
| Ingredient Quality | 19.0/20 |
| Texture | 14.6/15 |
| Cultural Authenticity | 19.6/20 |
| Value | 14.4/15 |

Sate Lilit has nothing in common with the peanut sauce skewers tourists expect when they hear "satay." Minced fish, chicken, or pork is mixed with grated coconut, kaffir lime leaves, lemongrass, and a complex blend of Balinese spices. The mixture is then hand wrapped around a lemongrass stalk or bamboo stick and grilled over charcoal.

The texture is remarkably smooth compared to conventional satay. The coconut adds a subtle richness, the lime leaf contributes a bright citrus edge, and the lemongrass handle infuses the meat from the inside. Fish versions tend to be lighter and more aromatic, while pork versions deliver deeper flavor and a slightly denser bite.

**Where we tested it:** Warung Lesehan Merta Sari (Klungkung), Gianyar Night Market, Warung Wardani (Denpasar), various roadside stalls in Ubud

**Best version:** Warung Lesehan Merta Sari in Klungkung has been making sate lilit since the 1990s. The fish version here is the benchmark: smooth, aromatic, and grilled to a light char that adds smokiness without drying the meat.

**Average price:** IDR 5,000 to IDR 15,000 per portion ($0.30 to $0.95 USD)

**Pro tip:** Sate Lilit works better as a side dish alongside Nasi Campur than as a standalone meal. Order it as an addition to your main plate.

### 4. Ayam Betutu (Slow Cooked Spiced Chicken)

**Overall Score: 96.0/100**

| Category | Score |
|---|---|
| Flavor Complexity | 29.0/30 |
| Ingredient Quality | 18.8/20 |
| Texture | 14.2/15 |
| Cultural Authenticity | 19.6/20 |
| Value | 14.4/15 |

Ayam Betutu is Bali's answer to slow cooked comfort food. A whole chicken is rubbed inside and out with an aromatic spice blend of turmeric, galangal, shrimp paste, garlic, shallots, lemongrass, and ginger. The bird is wrapped in banana leaves and then slow roasted for a minimum of six to eight hours. Some traditional preparations bury the wrapped chicken in a pit of embers underground.

The result is chicken so tender it practically disintegrates at the touch of a fork. The spice paste penetrates deep into the meat, and the banana leaf wrapper adds a faint smoky sweetness that you cannot replicate through other cooking methods. Traditionally served with rice, water spinach, fried peanuts, and a devastatingly spicy sambal.

**Where we tested it:** Ayam Betutu Khas Gilimanuk (Kuta), Warung Liku (Denpasar), Gianyar Night Market

**Best version:** Ayam Betutu Khas Gilimanuk on Jalan Raya Tuban is the go to spot for most Balinese locals. The chicken is cooked until the spices have fully absorbed into every fiber, and the heat level is authentically aggressive. They also serve a duck version (Bebek Betutu) that scores equally well.

**Average price:** IDR 35,000 to IDR 60,000 per portion ($2.20 to $3.80 USD)

**Pro tip:** If you can handle serious heat, order the original spice level. Many tourist oriented versions dial back the chili, and you lose a significant amount of the dish's intended character.

### 5. Bebek Betutu (Smoked Duck)

**Overall Score: 95.6/100**

| Category | Score |
|---|---|
| Flavor Complexity | 28.6/30 |
| Ingredient Quality | 19.0/20 |
| Texture | 14.2/15 |

Bebek Betutu follows the same preparation philosophy as Ayam Betutu, but with duck. Local ducks are rubbed and stuffed with basa genep spices, wrapped in banana leaves and coconut husks, then traditionally cooked in a pit of smoldering embers for up to 24 hours.

The duck version delivers a richer, gamier flavor profile than chicken. The extended cooking time renders the fat completely, leaving meat that has absorbed the full spectrum of the spice paste. When done correctly, the meat practically melts off the bone with a lingering smoky finish that stays with you for hours.

**Where we tested it:** Bebek Bengil (Ubud), Warung Liku (Denpasar), Art Cafe Bumbu Bali (Nusa Dua)

**Best version:** Bebek Bengil in Ubud (also known as the "Dirty Duck Diner") lives up to its reputation. The duck meat is extraordinarily tender, and the char on the banana leaf wrapper adds a layer of smokiness that most competitors cannot match.

**Average price:** IDR 55,000 to IDR 90,000 per portion ($3.50 to $5.70 USD)

**Pro tip:** Order the Bebek Betutu, not the crispy fried duck. Many restaurants push the fried version to tourists, but the slow smoked preparation is the authentic and superior dish.

### 6. Lawar (Traditional Balinese Salad)

**Overall Score: 95.2/100**

| Category | Score |
|---|---|
| Flavor Complexity | 28.4/30 |
| Ingredient Quality | 19.0/20 |
| Texture | 14.4/15 |
| Cultural Authenticity | 19.4/20 |
| Value | 14.0/15 |

Lawar is deeply tied to Balinese Hindu ceremonies and is one of the most culturally significant dishes on the island. It consists of finely chopped vegetables (commonly young jackfruit, long beans, or banana flower), grated coconut, minced meat (usually pork or chicken), and a generous mix of fresh spices. Traditional versions sometimes include animal blood to deepen the flavor, though many warungs skip this for tourist palates.

The texture is unlike anything else on this list. The grated coconut gives it a slightly grainy, rich body, while the fresh vegetables provide crunch and the spices deliver a warm, earthy heat. It is most commonly served as a side dish alongside Babi Guling or as part of a Nasi Campur plate.

**Where we tested it:** Every Babi Guling warung on this list serves lawar as a standard accompaniment.

**Best version:** The lawar served alongside Babi Guling at Pak Malen in Seminyak was consistently the most well balanced. The coconut was freshly grated, the vegetables retained their crunch, and the spice blend had real depth.

**Average price:** Typically included with Babi Guling or Nasi Campur plates. Standalone orders range from IDR 15,000 to IDR 25,000 ($0.95 to $1.60 USD).

**Pro tip:** Ask for "lawar merah" (red lawar) for the traditional version with blood. "Lawar putih" (white lawar) is the version without.

### 7. Sambal Matah (Raw Balinese Chili Relish)

**Overall Score: 95.0/100**

| Category | Score |
|---|---|
| Flavor Complexity | 28.6/30 |
| Ingredient Quality | 19.0/20 |
| Texture | 14.2/15 |
| Cultural Authenticity | 19.2/20 |
| Value | 14.0/15 |

Sambal Matah is not just a condiment. In Bali, it is a standalone culinary experience that shows up on virtually every table. Raw shallots, lemongrass, garlic, chili, and kaffir lime juice are finely chopped and mixed with hot coconut oil, salt, and a squeeze of lime. The "matah" means raw, and that rawness is exactly what makes it special.

Unlike cooked sambals, Sambal Matah delivers a bright, sharp, almost electric heat that hits differently from the first bite. The lemongrass adds a fragrance that lingers, the shallots provide crunch, and the coconut oil ties everything together into a cohesive relish that elevates any protein it touches.

**Where we tested it:** Present at virtually every warung and restaurant across Bali.

**Best version:** The Sambal Matah at Warung Men Weti in Sanur was the most balanced we found. Fresh, punchy, and made that morning.

**Average price:** Included with most dishes. Standalone portions cost IDR 5,000 to IDR 10,000 ($0.30 to $0.65 USD).

**Pro tip:** If a warung's Sambal Matah is good, everything else on their menu is likely good too. It is the single most reliable quality indicator in Balinese cooking.

### 8. Nasi Goreng (Balinese Fried Rice)

**Overall Score: 94.6/100**

| Category | Score |
|---|---|
| Flavor Complexity | 28.2/30 |
| Ingredient Quality | 18.8/20 |
| Texture | 14.2/15 |
| Cultural Authenticity | 18.8/20 |
| Value | 14.6/15 |

Nasi Goreng is the national dish of Indonesia, and the Balinese version carries a spicier, more aromatic signature than what you will find in Jakarta or Yogyakarta. Day old rice is wok fried with kecap manis (sweet soy sauce), garlic, shallots, chili, and shrimp paste, then topped with a fried egg, kerupuk, sliced cucumber, and your choice of protein.

What separates a great Nasi Goreng from a forgettable one is the wok heat. The rice needs to hit a properly seasoned, smoking hot wok to develop that slightly charred, nutty flavor that defines the dish. Warungs that cook over gas burners at high heat consistently outperform restaurants using electric stoves.

**Where we tested it:** Warung Varuna (Canggu), Froggy's Canggu, various street stalls across Ubud and Denpasar

**Best version:** Warung Varuna in Canggu nails the fundamentals. The rice arrives with visible wok char, the sambal is house made, and a full plate costs under IDR 35,000.

**Average price:** IDR 20,000 to IDR 45,000 per plate ($1.25 to $2.85 USD)

**Pro tip:** Order "nasi goreng spesial" for the version with a fried egg on top and extra kerupuk. The fried egg yolk mixed into the rice is a textural game changer.

### 9. Pepes Ikan (Spiced Fish in Banana Leaf)

**Overall Score: 94.2/100**

| Category | Score |
|---|---|
| Flavor Complexity | 28.2/30 |
| Ingredient Quality | 18.6/20 |
| Texture | 14.2/15 |
| Cultural Authenticity | 19.0/20 |

Pepes Ikan is a preparation method as much as it is a dish. Fresh fish (commonly mackerel, tuna, or snapper) is marinated with turmeric, chili, lemongrass, shallots, and other local spices, then wrapped tightly in banana leaves and either grilled or steamed.

The banana leaf acts as both a cooking vessel and a flavor agent. It traps moisture inside, keeping the fish impossibly tender, while simultaneously imparting a subtle earthy, vegetal sweetness to the exterior. When the leaf is unwrapped at the table, the burst of fragrant steam is one of the best sensory experiences in all of Balinese food.

**Where we tested it:** Jimbaran seafood market stalls, various warungs in Sanur and Ubud

**Best version:** The grilled Pepes Ikan at the Jimbaran beachfront stalls, where the fish is cooked over coconut husk charcoal, delivered the most pronounced smoky flavor in our testing.

**Average price:** IDR 30,000 to IDR 55,000 per piece ($1.90 to $3.50 USD)

**Pro tip:** Grilled Pepes Ikan develops more flavor complexity than steamed. Always choose the grilled option when available.

### 10. Gado Gado (Vegetable Salad with Peanut Sauce)

**Overall Score: 93.8/100**

Gado Gado is Indonesia's most famous vegetarian dish, and the Balinese version stands out for its freshness and the quality of the peanut sauce. Steamed vegetables (cabbage, long beans, bean sprouts, spinach), tofu, tempeh, and hard boiled eggs are arranged on a plate and smothered in a thick, slightly sweet peanut sauce.

The peanut sauce is where the dish succeeds or fails. The best versions are made from freshly roasted peanuts ground with garlic, chili, tamarind, palm sugar, and a squeeze of lime. The texture should be thick enough to coat the vegetables without pooling at the bottom of the plate. The contrast between the crunchy bean sprouts, the creamy sauce, and the firm boiled egg makes this one of the most satisfying plant forward dishes in Southeast Asia.

**Where we tested it:** Various warungs in Denpasar, Ubud, and Canggu

**Best version:** Warung Wardani in Denpasar serves a textbook Gado Gado with a peanut sauce that is clearly made fresh daily. The vegetable selection was the most diverse we encountered.

**Average price:** IDR 20,000 to IDR 35,000 per plate ($1.25 to $2.20 USD)

**Pro tip:** Ask for extra kerupuk on the side. The prawn crackers add a salty, crunchy element that rounds out the dish perfectly.

### 11. Bakso (Indonesian Meatball Soup)

**Overall Score: 93.4/100**

| Category | Score |
|---|---|
| Flavor Complexity | 27.6/30 |
| Ingredient Quality | 18.6/20 |
| Texture | 14.0/15 |

Bakso has Chinese roots but has become one of the most beloved street foods across all of Indonesia. In Bali, you will find it sold from small carts towed by motorbike, at dedicated bakso restaurants, and at virtually every night market.

The dish features bouncy chicken or beef meatballs served in a clear, savory broth with rice noodles, tofu, simple greens, and sometimes dumplings or wontons. The meatballs have a distinctive springy texture that comes from the addition of tapioca starch to the meat mixture. A good bowl is warming, deeply savory, and endlessly customizable with the chili sauce, soy sauce, and vinegar condiments that sit on every bakso table.

**Where we tested it:** Bakso IGA (Canggu), street vendors in Ubud, Gianyar Night Market

**Best version:** Bakso IGA in Canggu offers a foreigner friendly introduction with excellent meatball texture and a rich, clean broth. For the more adventurous, the motorbike cart vendors in Ubud serve versions with hand formed meatballs that have more rustic character.

**Average price:** IDR 15,000 to IDR 35,000 per bowl ($0.95 to $2.20 USD)

**Pro tip:** If you are new to Bali and concerned about food safety, start with an established bakso restaurant rather than a street cart. Once your stomach has adjusted, explore the street versions.

**Overall Score: 93.0/100**

Nasi Jinggo is Bali's cheapest and most democratic meal. A small portion of steamed rice is paired with one or two side dishes (shredded spiced chicken, noodles, tempeh, egg, or sambal), wrapped tightly in a banana leaf, and sold from the back of motorbikes and roadside stalls, primarily in the evening and late at night.

Each packet costs between IDR 5,000 and IDR 10,000 (roughly $0.30 to $0.65 USD), making it the most affordable complete meal on the island. The portions are small by design. Locals typically buy two or three packets at a time, mixing and matching the fillings.

**Where we tested it:** Various roadside vendors in Denpasar, Ubud, and Canggu (late night)

**Best version:** The Nasi Jinggo vendors clustered near Pasar Badung in Denpasar offer the widest selection of fillings and the most consistent quality. Look for the stalls with the longest lines of locals.

**Average price:** IDR 5,000 to IDR 10,000 per packet ($0.30 to $0.65 USD)

**Pro tip:** Buy three or four packets with different fillings and eat them together. This gives you a Nasi Campur style experience at a fraction of the price.

## Which Bali Food Should You Try First?

Your priorities and appetite will determine what you chase first. Here is our scenario based breakdown.

**First time in Bali?** Go straight to Babi Guling. It is the island's most iconic dish, and eating it at a proper warung before 11:30 AM is a foundational Bali experience. Pak Malen in Seminyak or Ibu Oka in Ubud are both proven starting points.

**Vegetarian or vegan?** Gado Gado and Nasi Campur are your best options. Most warungs will happily build a meatless Nasi Campur plate using tempeh, tofu, vegetables, and peanuts. Gado Gado is vegetarian by default and available everywhere.

**On a tight budget?** Nasi Jinggo is the most food per rupiah on the island. Buying three or four packets with different fillings for under IDR 30,000 total gives you a complete, diverse meal. Bakso from street carts is another excellent budget option.

**Love spicy food?** Ayam Betutu at full spice level is the ultimate test. Order from Ayam Betutu Khas Gilimanuk and request the original heat level. Follow that with a plate of Nasi Campur loaded with Sambal Matah and you will have experienced Balinese heat at its most authentic.

**Traveling with kids?** Nasi Goreng and Bakso are the safest introductions. Both are mild by default, familiar in concept (fried rice and meatball soup), and available at every restaurant on the island. Ask for "tidak pedas" (not spicy) when ordering.

**Exploring at night?** Head to Gianyar Night Market. It is approximately 30 minutes from Ubud by car or scooter, and it is where Balinese families actually eat in the evening. Babi Guling stalls, sate lilit grilled over charcoal, unwrapped Ayam Betutu, traditional cakes, and market snacks that never appear on restaurant menus. Everything is cooked fresh in front of you. Prices are fixed. The atmosphere is local, loud, and completely genuine.

## Bali Food Budget Guide

One of the best things about eating in Bali is how far your money stretches. Here is a realistic daily food budget based on our testing.

**Budget traveler (eating at warungs and street stalls):** IDR 100,000 to IDR 200,000 per day ($6.30 to $12.60 USD). This covers three solid meals plus snacks and drinks. You will eat extraordinarily well at this price point.

**Mid range traveler (mix of warungs and casual restaurants):** IDR 200,000 to IDR 400,000 per day ($12.60 to $25.20 USD). This lets you try warung food for lunch and eat at a nicer cafe or restaurant for dinner.

**Comfort traveler (restaurants and upscale dining):** IDR 400,000+ per day ($25.20+ USD). This covers mid range restaurants for most meals with the option of one high end dinner during your trip.

For context, a full day of eating the dishes on this list exclusively at local warungs would cost approximately IDR 120,000 to IDR 180,000 ($7.50 to $11.30 USD). That includes a Babi Guling lunch, Nasi Campur breakfast, Sate Lilit snack, and a Bakso dinner with drinks.

## Where to Eat in Bali: Region Guide

**Ubud:** The cultural heart of Bali and the best region for Babi Guling (Ibu Oka, Gung Cung), Bebek Betutu (Bebek Bengil), and refined Nasi Campur. Prices are slightly higher than Denpasar due to tourist demand, but quality is consistently strong.

**Denpasar:** The actual capital and where Balinese locals eat. Warung Wardani and Warung Liku are local institutions. Prices are the lowest on the island, and the food is among the most authentic. Less tourist infrastructure, but far better value.

**Canggu:** Heavy on Western style cafes and brunch spots, but excellent warungs exist if you know where to look. Warung Varuna and Warung Sika both serve reliable traditional food at fair prices.

**Seminyak:** More upscale dining options and the home of Pak Malen for Babi Guling. Good for mixing high end restaurant experiences with warung visits. Prices run higher than Denpasar and Canggu.

**Jimbaran:** Famous for beachfront seafood grilling. This is where you eat Pepes Ikan and grilled fish while watching the sunset. Prices are tourist oriented but the experience justifies the premium.

**Gianyar:** Home to the best night market in Bali and several excellent Babi Guling warungs including Pande Egi, which serves roast pig with rice field views. Worth the 30 minute drive from Ubud.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Food in Bali

**What is the single best food to eat in Bali?**

Babi Guling (roasted suckling pig) earned the highest score in our evaluation at 97.8/100. It is the island's signature dish and the one meal every visitor should prioritize. The combination of crispy skin, tender spiced meat, and fiery sambal served over rice delivers the most complete representation of Balinese flavors in a single plate.

**Is Bali street food safe to eat?**

Yes, with reasonable precautions. Stick to stalls and warungs with high customer turnover, as this ensures fresh cooking and less time for food to sit. Avoid raw salads at questionable stalls. Drink bottled water. Most travelers experience no issues when following these basic guidelines. If you are in your first few days, start at established warungs before exploring street carts.

**How much does food cost in Bali?**

Bali is one of the most affordable food destinations in Southeast Asia. A complete meal at a local warung costs IDR 20,000 to IDR 50,000 ($1.25 to $3.15 USD). Street food snacks run IDR 5,000 to IDR 15,000 ($0.30 to $0.95 USD). Mid range restaurants charge IDR 80,000 to IDR 200,000 ($5 to $12.60 USD) per dish. Fine dining runs IDR 400,000+ ($25+ USD). A full day of excellent eating at warungs can cost as little as IDR 120,000 ($7.50 USD).

**Is Bali food halal friendly?**

Bali is a Hindu majority island, so pork is prevalent in traditional Balinese cuisine. However, halal food is widely available, particularly in areas with larger Muslim populations like Denpasar. Many restaurants serve chicken, fish, and vegetable based dishes that comply with halal requirements. Warung Wardani in Denpasar is known for serving halal Balinese food. Always ask your server if you are unsure about a specific dish.

**What is the difference between Balinese food and Indonesian food?**

Balinese cuisine is a regional subset of Indonesian cooking with distinct characteristics. It relies heavily on basa genep (a complex spice paste), uses more pork due to the Hindu majority population, tends to run spicier than Javanese food, and features unique preparations like Babi Guling, Lawar, and Sate Lilit that are specific to the island. Indonesian staples like Nasi Goreng and Bakso appear on Balinese menus but often with a heavier hand on turmeric, chili, and lemongrass.

**What time should I eat at warungs for the best food?**

For Babi Guling, arrive before 11:30 AM. For Nasi Campur, breakfast and lunch service (7 AM to 1 PM) is when ingredients are freshest. Night markets typically start around 5 PM and run until late evening. Many warungs close once their daily preparation sells out, so earlier is almost always better for traditional Balinese dishes.

**Should I take a food tour in Bali?**

A guided food tour is worth it for first time visitors, particularly in Denpasar or Ubud. Tours provide access to warungs and stalls you would likely miss on your own, cultural context about what you are eating, and guidance on food safety. Budget approximately IDR 500,000 to IDR 1,000,000 ($31 to $63 USD) per person for a quality half day tour. The Denpasar food tour route tends to cover more authentic local spots than the more tourist oriented Ubud tours.

**Can I eat well in Bali as a vegetarian?**

Absolutely. Gado Gado, Nasi Campur (vegetarian build), tipat cantok (rice cake salad with peanut sauce), and various tempeh and tofu preparations are available at nearly every warung. Ubud and Canggu also have large concentrations of dedicated vegetarian and vegan restaurants. Bali is one of the most vegetarian friendly destinations in Southeast Asia.

**What is a warung?**

A warung is a small, family run Indonesian restaurant. Think of it as the local equivalent of a neighborhood diner. Warungs serve traditional home style food at very low prices in a no frills setting. The food is prepared daily according to family recipes that are often passed down across generations. Eating at warungs is the single most effective way to experience authentic Balinese food culture, and it is where the vast majority of locals eat every day.

**What dishes should I avoid as a first time visitor?**

Nothing on this list should be avoided, but approach Lawar with blood (lawar merah) cautiously if you have a sensitive stomach. Also be aware that some Babi Guling plates include offal and blood sausage as part of the standard serving. These are traditional components and perfectly safe to eat, but they can surprise first time visitors who are not expecting them. When in doubt, ask your server to explain what is on your plate before eating.
